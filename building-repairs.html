Building and repairs

Fix the home safely, document the work, and rebuild stronger.

This page turns cleanup, inspections, permits, substantial damage review, and flood-resilience upgrades into a practical resident checklist.

Immediate fixes Rebuild requirements Resilience upgrades
Start here Safety before repair.

Do not enter unsafe water, energize wet electrical systems, or run flooded HVAC until qualified professionals clear the conditions.

Permit gate Ask before rebuilding.

Floodplain repairs can trigger permits, inspections, substantial damage review, and elevation documentation.

Local office Use the correct municipality.

Permits are usually handled by the city or township where the property sits. If unsure, start with your local building department and ask for the floodplain administrator.

Stop work if

You smell gas, see structural movement, have standing water near electrical equipment, see sewage contamination, or feel sick during cleanup.

Call first

Insurance, local building department, floodplain administrator, electrician, HVAC professional, plumber, and mitigation contractor as needed.

Save proof

Photos, videos, water lines, serial numbers, receipts, estimates, permits, inspection reports, and before-removal photos.

Phase 2

Rebuild Requirements

Before major repair or reconstruction, residents should confirm which approval gates apply. Floodplain work may involve local permits, Michigan floodplain rules, and substantial damage decisions.

Contact the local building department

Ask which permits are needed for structural repair, electrical, mechanical, plumbing, drywall replacement, demolition, basement work, utility relocation, grading, or work in a floodplain.

Ask whether floodplain review applies

Michigan requires permits before alteration or occupation of the 100-year floodplain of a river, stream, lake, or drain. Local floodplain administrators help determine what applies to the property.

Prepare for substantial damage review

If repair cost reaches the substantial damage threshold, the building may need to be brought into floodplain/building-code compliance before reconstruction continues.

Collect elevation information

Ask whether an elevation certificate, lowest-floor elevation, base flood elevation, or local design flood elevation is needed before plans are approved.

Get itemized contractor estimates

Separate emergency cleanup, code-required safety work, structural repair, utilities, contents, and mitigation upgrades so insurance and permit reviewers can understand the scope.

Phase 3

Resilience Upgrades

These upgrades reduce repeat damage. The right choice depends on flood source, elevation, basement/crawlspace conditions, sewer backup risk, lot drainage, and local code approval.

Backflow valves

Help reduce sewage or drain backup through plumbing lines. Needs professional sizing, maintenance, and code-compliant installation.

Sump pump backup

Add battery or water-powered backup, alarms, discharge maintenance, and a plan for long power outages.

Raised utilities

Move panels, HVAC, water heaters, controls, and outlets above expected flood levels where practical and permitted.

Flood-resistant materials

Use materials below risk elevations that can resist water, be cleaned, and dry faster after flooding.

Grading and drainage

Route water away from the foundation, maintain gutters/downspouts, correct low spots, and keep drains clear.

Protected mechanical systems

Install platforms, flood-resistant supports, shutoff access, and service clearances for critical systems.

Best upgrade sequence: first stop water entry where possible, then protect utilities, then use materials that tolerate water, then improve drainage and backup power.

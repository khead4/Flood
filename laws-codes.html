Current rules residents need translated

Flood regulations are not one simple checklist. They come from federal minimums, model building standards, state/local enforcement, and sometimes stricter community rules. The UX problem is that residents rarely know which layer applies to them.

LayerWhat it controlsResident-facing translationWhere it can improve
NFIP minimumsBasic floodplain performance requirements tied to FEMA flood maps and community participation.“This is the minimum standard your community must enforce in mapped flood hazard areas.”Minimums can under-communicate residual risk and do not always reflect future rainfall or urban drainage flooding.
ASCE 24 + I-CodesFlood-resistant design and construction methods for structures subject to building code requirements.“This explains how buildings should be designed, elevated, vented, and protected.”Residents need a simplified pathway from technical terms to actual choices: elevation, vents, materials, utilities, drainage.
Michigan + local ordinancePermitting, substantial damage/improvement decisions, and local floodplain enforcement.“Your local official determines what you must do before repair or construction.”Better public tools could show when repair work triggers compliance and what documents are needed before rebuilding.
Higher standardsFreeboard, cumulative substantial improvement rules, stricter drainage, and climate-aware assumptions.“The safest option may be more protective than the legal minimum.”Communities can reduce repeat loss by making safer standards easier to understand and easier to choose.
UX insight: the page should not only list rules. It should explain what a homeowner does next based on location, damage, repair cost, and risk level.

Mapped floodplain

Check FEMA flood maps and local map repositories, then confirm with the local floodplain administrator before relying on assumptions.

Substantial damage / improvement

If damage or work reaches the local threshold, the structure may need to be brought into floodplain and building-code compliance before work proceeds.

Permits before rebuilding

Work in floodplains, wetlands, waterways, drains, or regulated shorelines may require local and/or state review before construction.

Where current guidance can improve

Make risk legible beyond the map

Many users interpret “not in a mapped flood zone” as “not at risk.” The interface should explain rainfall, basement backup, storm sewer overload, shoreline surge, and drainage patterns separately.

Turn standards into decision paths

Users need to know whether they should elevate, wet floodproof, relocate utilities, install backflow protection, improve drainage, or contact an engineer.

Show approval gates early

Before users spend money, the guide should tell them which work usually requires a permit, elevation certificate, engineering review, or local floodplain approval.

Important: This prototype is educational and UX-focused. Property owners should confirm requirements with their local floodplain administrator, building official, licensed architect, engineer, or surveyor before construction.

Immediate fixes playbook

Do the first repairs in the right order.

This page expands water extraction, electrical inspection, HVAC checks, mold prevention, debris removal, and safe access into practical steps, materials, contacts, questions, and documents.

Core idea A fix is a workflow, not a label.

Residents need the order, contacts, materials, documentation, and decision points that let them actually complete the work.

Start here

The order matters.

Use this sequence so people are safe, the home is not made worse, and documents are preserved for insurance, aid, permits, and contractors.

1Safe accessCan anyone enter without electrical, gas, structural, sewage, or health danger? 2Document firstRecord water lines, rooms, systems, exterior source, and hazards before removal when safe. 3Call the right peopleEmergency, utility, insurer, mitigation, electrician, HVAC, plumber, building office. 4Perform the fixExtract, inspect, dry, remove debris, prevent mold, and preserve proof.

Safety gate

Stop first if any of these are true

Do not enter or work yet

  • Standing water is near electrical panels, outlets, extension cords, appliances, or meters.
  • You smell gas, fuel, chemicals, sewage, or strong odors that make people sick.
  • Floors, stairs, porches, ceilings, foundations, or walls look unstable.
  • Water is still rising, moving, or pushing against foundation walls.
  • Children, older adults, medically vulnerable people, or pets would be exposed to contaminated water.

Call before continuing

  • Emergency services for life safety, trapped people, evacuation, or immediate medical risk.
  • Utility provider for electric or gas hazards.
  • Licensed electrician for wet electrical systems.
  • HVAC professional before running flooded heating or cooling equipment.
  • Plumber or public works contact for sewer backup, drain backup, sump, or backflow concerns.
  • Building department if access, structure, permits, or habitability are uncertain.
Do not energize wet systems. Wet electrical, HVAC, gas, fuel, and mechanical systems need qualified inspection before use. The wrong order can injure people and can also damage the claim record.

Before removal

Build the proof before you build the repair plan

Documentation is the bridge between the emergency, the repair, insurance, aid, and code review. It should happen before disposal whenever safety allows.

1Wide photosEach room, each wall, exterior water, drains, yard, street, basement, garage, and access points.
2Close photosWater lines, damaged materials, outlets, panels, HVAC, appliances, serial numbers, labels, and contents.
3TimelineWhen rain started, when water entered, when utilities were shut off, who was called, and what they said.
4Cost recordEvery receipt, deposit, estimate, invoice, rental, hotel, disposal charge, and emergency supply.

Fix playbooks

Each immediate fix, expanded into what the resident actually needs

Immediate fix

Water extraction

Goal: remove standing water and start drying without creating electrical, structural, or claim-documentation problems.

Check before starting
  • Power is off or cleared by a qualified person.
  • Water is not actively rising.
  • Foundation pressure, sewage, and chemical contamination are considered.
  • Photos are taken before pumping or moving items when safe.
Materials or support
  • Mitigation company, pump, wet/dry extraction, hoses, dehumidifiers, air movers, moisture meter readings.
  • PPE, buckets, labeled bins, plastic sheeting, trash bags, and safe extension power only after electrical clearance.
Ask
  • Can extraction begin before adjuster inspection?
  • Will the contractor provide drying logs and moisture readings?
  • What materials should be removed vs dried?
Save
  • Before/after photos, water depth, water source notes, contractor invoice, drying log, moisture readings, equipment rental receipts.
Do not
  • Do not use electrical equipment in wet areas until cleared.
  • Do not discard damaged items without photos unless keeping them is unsafe.
  • Do not assume pumped water solves mold or structural moisture.

Immediate fix

Electrical inspection

Goal: confirm panels, outlets, wiring, meters, appliances, and connected systems are safe before power is used.

Check before starting
  • Whether water touched the panel, outlets, wiring, meter, breakers, appliance cords, sump, furnace, or basement equipment.
  • Whether anyone has turned power back on.
  • Whether the utility provider gave restoration instructions.
Materials or support
  • Licensed electrician, utility provider, building inspector where required, replacement outlets/breakers/wiring/components as determined by the professional.
Ask
  • What must stay off until repaired?
  • Is a permit or inspection required?
  • Can you provide a written safety report for the insurer and packet?
Save
  • Photos from a safe distance, electrician report, repair estimate, utility notice, permit/inspection record, paid invoice.
Do not
  • Do not stand in water to touch breakers or appliances.
  • Do not run extension cords through wet areas.
  • Do not let power restoration outrun inspection.

Immediate fix

HVAC, furnace, boiler, water heater, and duct checks

Goal: prevent unsafe operation, contaminated air movement, fuel hazards, and hidden system damage.

Check before starting
  • Water depth around equipment.
  • Whether burners, motors, controls, insulation, ductwork, or fuel lines were affected.
  • Whether the system was run after flooding.
Materials or support
  • Licensed HVAC contractor, plumber for water heater/boiler, fuel or gas provider where needed, replacement estimate if equipment is unsafe or not cleanable.
Ask
  • Is the system safe to run?
  • Can it be cleaned, repaired, or must it be replaced?
  • Can equipment be raised or protected during replacement?
Save
  • Model and serial photos, water-line photos, service report, replacement estimate, repair invoice, notes on raised-utility options.
Do not
  • Do not run flooded HVAC to dry the house.
  • Do not ignore contaminated ducts or soaked insulation.
  • Do not replace equipment without asking whether elevation or protection is required or recommended.

Immediate fix

Mold prevention and moisture control

Goal: dry the structure, remove materials that cannot be safely cleaned, and document mitigation before mold becomes a larger health and claim issue.

Check before starting
  • How long materials have been wet.
  • Whether water may contain sewage or chemicals.
  • Which materials are porous, absorbent, or trapped behind walls/floors.
  • Whether household members have asthma, immune conditions, or mold sensitivity.
Materials or support
  • Mitigation contractor, PPE, ventilation when safe, dehumidifiers, moisture readings, containment, cleaning supplies appropriate to the material and contamination level.
Ask
  • What should be removed vs cleaned?
  • Are moisture readings documented?
  • Is professional mold cleanup needed?
Save
  • Photos of wet materials before removal, moisture readings, removed-material list, cleanup receipts, contractor report, PPE/supply receipts.
Do not
  • Do not cover wet materials with new flooring or drywall.
  • Do not run contaminated HVAC.
  • Do not rely on odor alone to decide whether materials are dry.

Immediate fix

Debris removal and damaged-item sorting

Goal: remove unsafe debris while preserving enough proof to support insurance, aid, disposal, and replacement decisions.

Check before starting
  • Whether items are contaminated, sharp, heavy, or structurally connected.
  • Whether the insurer wants inspection before disposal.
  • Whether local disposal rules apply to electronics, appliances, chemicals, fuel, or construction debris.
Materials or support
  • Gloves, eye protection, masks/respirators appropriate to conditions, heavy bags, labels, bins, tarps, hauling service, disposal guidance.
Ask
  • Can unsalvageable items be discarded after photos?
  • Does debris need to be separated by type?
  • Can the hauler provide weight tickets or receipts?
Save
  • Photos of every pile, item list, room location, serial numbers, haul receipts, disposal fees, date removed.
Do not
  • Do not mix hazardous materials with regular debris.
  • Do not throw away proof before photographing.
  • Do not let debris block safe access paths or utilities.

Immediate fix

Safe access documentation

Goal: prove why a home, room, basement, driveway, or utility area cannot be accessed or occupied safely.

Check before starting
  • Blocked doors, unsafe stairs, wet electrical paths, sewage, collapsed ceilings, foundation cracks, unstable floors, or inaccessible utilities.
Materials or support
  • Photos, short videos, written notes, inspector or contractor report, temporary barrier/signage if appropriate, lodging record if displaced.
Ask
  • Is the home safe to enter?
  • Is it safe to occupy?
  • What must be fixed before re-entry?
  • Can the unsafe condition be documented in writing?
Save
  • Videos of blocked access, inspection notes, unsafe-condition letters, hotel/lodging reason, photos of hazards, dates access changed.
Do not
  • Do not let people re-enter because paperwork is inconvenient.
  • Do not remove barriers or warnings before hazards are cleared.
  • Do not forget to document why hotel or temporary housing was necessary.

Materials and records

What people usually need to complete the immediate-fix workflow

Personal protectionGloves, eye protection, protective clothing, sturdy footwear, and respiratory protection appropriate to the hazard.
DocumentationPhone/camera, charger, measuring tape, notebook, waterproof marker, labels, folders, cloud backup, and receipts envelope.
Sorting and removalBins, bags, tarps, labels, hand truck, disposal plan, haul receipts, and separation for hazardous or special materials.
Drying supportProfessional mitigation, pumps, extraction equipment, fans/air movers, dehumidifiers, moisture readings, and drying logs after electrical clearance.
Professional reportsElectrician, HVAC, plumber, mitigation, inspector, structural, and contractor reports depending on the hazard.
Cost trackingEstimates, invoices, deposits, paid-up-front costs, reimbursement notes, denied items, and still-needed payments.

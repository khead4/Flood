A resilient home strategy usually combines elevation, protected utilities, flood openings, backflow prevention, and site drainage.

Build or edit a home for flood resilience

The safest design strategy is layered. A home should reduce the chance of water entry, limit damage if water enters, protect life-safety systems, and recover faster after the event.

If the home does not meet current status quo

Start by identifying whether the issue is elevation, enclosure, utilities, materials, drainage, or permits. Then separate emergency fixes from code-triggering structural work.

Document the current state

Photos, utility locations, basement conditions, elevations, visible water lines, and known flood history.

Ask what rule applies

Local officials can confirm flood zone, substantial damage/improvement triggers, and permit requirements.

Choose the least confusing upgrade path

For some homes this is utility elevation; for others it is flood openings, backflow prevention, site drainage, or full elevation.

Recommended design principles

Dry where possible, wet where necessary. Keep living space and critical systems above flood levels, while designing lower-risk zones to resist, drain, and dry.

Warning: Dry floodproofing a residence is limited by code and hydrostatic pressure risks; it should not be treated as a simple DIY seal-the-house solution.

Resident-facing retrofit roadmap

Low disruption

Clean gutters/drains, regrade soil, extend downspouts, maintain sump systems, install alarms, move valuables above expected flood level.

Moderate construction

Install backflow valves, elevate utilities, use flood-resistant materials below risk elevations, add flood openings where required.

Major mitigation

Elevate the home, relocate it, rebuild noncompliant areas, redesign drainage, or redesign lower enclosures to comply with floodplain rules.

Important: This prototype is educational and UX-focused. Property owners should confirm requirements with their local floodplain administrator, building official, licensed architect, engineer, or surveyor before construction.

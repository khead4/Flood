Prepare

Make the home harder to flood before the next storm.

No house can be promised flood-proof in every event. The goal is layered protection: safer utilities, less water entry, faster drying, fewer losses, and clearer documents when help is needed.

Before building or buying

Start with land, elevation, water path, and code triggers

1Check the mapsSearch FEMA flood maps, local GIS, elevation, drains, waterways, soil drainage, and impervious surfaces.
2Ask local officialsConfirm zoning, floodplain rules, building permits, drain/wetland constraints, and substantial improvement triggers.
3Design above minimumsUse freeboard where appropriate, raised utilities, drainage, flood-resistant materials, and protected mechanical systems.
4Budget ownership riskPrice flood insurance, sewer backup endorsement, sump/backflow maintenance, drainage fixes, and emergency savings.

Before a flood

Do the low-regret actions first

Move what mattersRaise documents, medications, photos, keepsakes, electronics, tools, and valuables out of basements and low storage.
Protect utilitiesKnow where shutoffs are. Keep HVAC, water heater, electrical, and sump/backflow records together.
Clean the water pathClear gutters, downspouts, window wells, yard drains, sump discharge, and safe surface drainage away from the foundation.
Stage suppliesFlashlights, batteries, chargers, waterproof boots, gloves, masks, bins, labels, tarps, towels, and document folders.
Check coverageFlood insurance, homeowners/renters coverage, sewer backup endorsement, contents coverage, deductibles, and temporary-housing gaps.
Take baseline photosPhotograph rooms, contents, utilities, serial numbers, exterior drains, downspouts, sump, and backflow devices before damage occurs.
Do this while calm: write down insurer, utility provider, plumber, electrician, HVAC contractor, city building office, and local emergency contacts before a storm.

During rain or warning

Use a short safety sequence

Protect people first

Move people and pets away from floodwater, wet electrical areas, blocked exits, and roads with standing water.

Stop avoidable damage only if safe

Move items higher, check sump operation from a safe place, redirect downspouts if safe, and keep water away from utilities.

Record the event

Take safe photos of rain, street flooding, exterior water direction, drains, water entry points, and time stamps.

Do not rush cleanup

If electricity, gas, sewage, chemicals, structure, or medical risk is present, wait for qualified help.

After water enters

Document, dry, inspect, then rebuild stronger

First 24 hours when safe

  • Photograph water lines, rooms, exterior source, drains, utilities, damaged contents, and receipts.
  • Call insurer and ask what can be removed before inspection.
  • Get electrical/HVAC/plumbing clearance before using wet systems.
  • Begin water extraction and drying only after safety gates are cleared.

First week

  • Track mitigation invoices, moisture readings, contractor reports, disposal receipts, hotel costs, and utility notices.
  • Ask the building office whether permits, inspections, or substantial damage review apply.
  • Separate floodwater, sewer backup, clean-water leak, roof/window intrusion, and seepage because coverage paths differ.

Prevent the next loss

Layer the improvements

Raise utilitiesMove panels, outlets, HVAC, water heater, boiler, fuel systems, and controls above expected flood level when feasible.
Add backflow and sump protectionBackflow valve, sump backup, battery, alarm, discharge route, and maintenance schedule.
Use flood-resistant materialsChoose lower-wall and floor materials that tolerate wetting and cleaning better than carpet, paper-faced drywall, or absorbent finishes.
Improve drainageGrade away from foundation, extend downspouts, protect window wells, maintain drains, add rain gardens or permeable areas where suitable.
Reduce basement contentsKeep replaceable storage low and essential/valuable contents high. Use shelves and waterproof bins.
Record improvementsSave permits, receipts, contractor scopes, photos, elevation certificates, sump/backflow maintenance, and insurance changes.
Best standard: the home should be able to lose power, receive heavy rain, and still keep people safe, utilities protected, water paths managed, and repair documentation ready.

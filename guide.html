Rule of the page Safety, proof, then money.

Protect people first, document damage before disposal when safe, then route insurance and assistance by the actual cause of loss.

Limits Confirm final decisions.

Insurers, local building officials, floodplain administrators, licensed professionals, and assistance agencies make final determinations.

If anyone is in immediate danger, call emergency services first. Do not wait for map data, insurance guidance, or paperwork if water is rising, exits are blocked, electricity may be energized, gas is smelled, or a medical issue is present.

Step 1

Emergency first: decide whether the situation is safe enough to handle yourself

Flood recovery starts with a safety decision. If the situation is unsafe, the correct next step is not a claim form. It is leaving the hazard, calling emergency services, and keeping people away from electricity, contaminated water, unstable structures, and blocked roads.

Situation: danger may still be active. Action: leave hazards and call for help. Proof later: time, water depth, photos when safe.

Leave or call for help now if

  • Water is rising or moving quickly.
  • Water is touching outlets, electrical panels, appliances, or extension cords.
  • You smell gas, hear hissing, or suspect a fuel leak.
  • The foundation, stairs, porch, deck, ceiling, or walls look shifted or unstable.
  • Floodwater may include sewage, chemicals, fuel, or sharp debris.
  • A person needs medical support, oxygen, medication, mobility help, or evacuation help.
  • Roads are flooded and travel is not safe.

Do not do these during active flooding

  • Do not walk into unknown-depth floodwater.
  • Do not drive through flooded roads.
  • Do not touch wet electrical equipment.
  • Do not run flooded HVAC, furnace, water heater, or appliances.
  • Do not pump a basement too quickly if outside water is still pressing against the foundation.
  • Do not begin major demolition before photos and insurer instructions unless safety requires immediate removal.
Emergency actions for the first hour
  1. Move people and pets to higher, dry ground.
  2. Call emergency services if life safety is at risk.
  3. If safe, shut off utilities only from a dry location. If the shutoff area is wet, wait for a qualified professional.
  4. Keep children, older adults, and people with health conditions away from floodwater and cleanup zones.
  5. Use text messages or written notes to record the time flooding started, water depth, weather conditions, and urgent needs.
  6. Once safe, take wide photos of each affected room before moving items.

Step 4

Document damage: make the evidence complete enough for insurance, aid, and repairs

Good documentation answers five questions: what happened, where it happened, when it happened, what was damaged, and what it cost to stabilize or replace.

Action: photograph and list damage before disposal when safe. Pair with: room, cause, value, receipt, and coverage guess. Destination: claim packet, aid request, contractor scope.

Photograph before cleanup when safe

  • Wide room photo from each corner.
  • Close photo of every damaged item.
  • Water line photo with a ruler, tape measure, or known object for scale.
  • Photo of exterior flooding, drains, ditches, gutters, sump discharge, and street conditions.
  • Photo of electrical panel, furnace, water heater, HVAC, washer/dryer, appliances, and mechanical systems.
  • Photo of labels, serial numbers, model numbers, and purchase tags.
  • Photo of receipts, invoices, estimates, notices, and letters.

Separate items into clear buckets

  • Building: walls, flooring, foundation, stairs, systems, built-ins.
  • Contents: furniture, clothing, electronics, appliances not built in.
  • Exterior: fence, yard, landscaping, detached structures, vehicles.
  • Mitigation: pumps, drying, cleanup, mold prevention, debris removal.
  • Living costs: hotel, meals, travel, storage, childcare, pet boarding.
  • Utilities: electric, gas, water, sewer, plumbing, HVAC, safety inspections.

Step 5

Insurance route: choose the right claim path by water source and structure type

Coverage usually depends on the cause of damage, the structure, the policy, endorsements, and exclusions. The guide should never tell residents they are definitely covered. It should help them ask the right questions and organize evidence.

Decision: identify the water source first. Action: route flood, homeowners/renters, backup endorsement, auto, or aid. Proof: water path, policy notes, photos, reports, receipts.

Common coverage cautions
  • Standard homeowners and renters policies generally do not cover outside rising floodwater.
  • Flood insurance and regular property insurance can cover different causes of water damage.
  • Contents coverage is separate from building coverage in many flood policies.
  • Basements often have limits and special rules under flood policies.
  • Temporary housing and additional living expenses are often not covered by NFIP flood insurance, so aid and other policies may matter.
  • Outdoor property, landscaping, decks, fences, detached structures, and yards may have limited or no coverage depending on the policy.
  • Coverage for mold can be limited and time-sensitive; document mitigation steps immediately.
  • If a claim is denied, ask for the written reason and the appeal or review process.

Step 6

Assistance is not one category. Electricity, heat, water, daycare, hotel costs, food, housing costs, repairs, and cleanup often require different documents and different contacts.

How to apply without losing track
  1. Choose the need first. Do not send the same generic message to every program.
  2. Collect proof of address, ID, household size, income if required, loss date, and damage photos.
  3. Keep each assistance type in its own folder: utilities, water/sewer, food, hotel, repairs, childcare, housing.
  4. Write down application dates, case numbers, names, and required follow-up.
  5. Use the packet builder to preserve documents even if a program asks for them later.

Step 8

Cleanup and health: reduce mold, contamination, and unsafe re-entry risks

Flood cleanup is a health and building-safety task. The resident goal is to dry, remove contamination, prevent mold, and avoid unsafe electricity or structural hazards while preserving documentation.

Before entering

  • Check for visible structural damage from outside.
  • Assume floodwater may be contaminated.
  • Wear protective clothing, gloves, eye protection, and respiratory protection appropriate to the task.
  • Do not enter if electricity may be energized in wet areas.
  • Ventilate if safe, but do not run contaminated HVAC.

Cleanup priorities

  • Remove standing water when safe and when doing so will not worsen structural pressure.
  • Remove wet porous materials that cannot be cleaned and dried safely.
  • Dry the building with ventilation, dehumidification, and professional mitigation where needed.
  • Separate contaminated materials from clean salvageable materials.
  • Document every disposal pile before removal.
When to seek professional help
  • Large mold areas or mold affecting HVAC systems.
  • Sewage contamination or chemical contamination.
  • Electrical, gas, furnace, boiler, water heater, or HVAC flooding.
  • Foundation movement, sagging floors, damaged stairs, or roof/ceiling concerns.
  • Health symptoms during cleanup or high-risk household members.

Step 12

Rebuild stronger: reduce the next loss, not just repair this one

The best recovery outcome is not only getting paid. It is making the home safer for the next storm. The platform should turn map risk, building damage, and contractor findings into a short resilience plan.

Raise utilitiesMove electrical, HVAC, plumbing, fuel, and mechanical systems above likely flood levels where feasible and permitted.
Backflow protectionEvaluate backflow valves, sewer backup prevention, sump pump condition, and battery backup.
Drainage and gradingMove water away from the foundation with grading, downspout extensions, swales, and drainage improvements.
Flood-resistant materialsUse materials that tolerate wetting in flood-prone areas when allowed by code and appropriate to the structure.
Openings and enclosuresWhere flood openings or enclosure rules apply, confirm requirements with local officials and qualified professionals.
Green infrastructureConsider rain gardens, permeable pavement, trees, and site practices that reduce runoff when appropriate.

Step 13

Community reporting and mutual aid: turn repeated flooding into shared action

Individual recovery matters, but repeated street flooding, sewer backup clusters, blocked drains, and inaccessible housing are community problems. The platform should collect reports in a way that helps residents, local officials, volunteers, and planners see patterns.

Request helpCleanup, food delivery, transportation, childcare, accessibility support, supplies, storage, or documentation support.
Offer helpVolunteer time, tools, pumps, dehumidifiers, rides, meals, translation, temporary storage, or local knowledge.
Report patternsStreet flooding, blocked drains, repeated backups, water depth, date/time, photos, and nearby infrastructure.
Share verified resourcesOnly share program links, phone numbers, shelters, and donation needs that can be checked.
Protect privacyDo not post medical details, insurance documents, account numbers, or exact household vulnerabilities publicly.
Close the loopMark requests as open, in progress, resolved, referred, or no longer needed.
What makes a useful community flood report
  • Date and time of flooding.
  • City or township and nearest cross streets.
  • Estimated water depth and duration.
  • Photos of water, drains, road closures, or backup points when safe.
  • Whether homes, roads, utilities, or basements were affected.
  • Whether this has happened before and how often.
  • Whether a drain, pump, ditch, sewer, or waterway appeared blocked or overwhelmed.

Step 14

Final packet checklist: collect everything into one usable file set

The packet should be useful to the resident, insurer, adjuster, contractor, caseworker, landlord, city/township official, or aid agency. It should not be a pretty summary. It should be complete enough to send.

Goal: make one file set that can be sent or printed. Action: organize evidence by purpose, not by random upload order. Result: summary, folders, inventory, costs, open questions.

Packet folders

  • 01 Resident and property information
  • 02 Location, rain, elevation, and map context
  • 03 Photos and videos
  • 04 Item inventory and estimated values
  • 05 Receipts, invoices, estimates, and payments
  • 06 Insurance claim notes and policy contacts
  • 07 Assistance applications and case numbers
  • 08 Utilities, water/sewer, plumbing, electrical, HVAC
  • 09 Building permits, inspections, contractor scopes
  • 10 Appeal, denial, or follow-up documents

Summary page should include

  • Resident name, address, phone, email, preferred contact.
  • Loss date, flood date, and when damage was discovered.
  • Cause of damage as understood so far.
  • Safe access concerns and utility hazards.
  • Total estimated damage, paid up front, likely reimbursement, still need to pay, and out-of-pocket exposure.
  • Missing documents and open questions.
  • Who the packet is being sent to and why.
Use the platform packet builder for these tasks
  1. Set the property location first so the packet knows the city and map context.
  2. Add photos, videos, receipts, estimates, notices, and reports.
  3. Classify each damaged item by room, item type, value, and likely coverage route.
  4. Enter paid-up-front costs and expected reimbursement percentage.
  5. Review the generated document preview.
  6. Download, print, or forward the packet to the right contact.

Step 15

Call scripts: what to say when you contact insurers, agencies, and officials

In a crisis, residents should not have to invent the right wording. These scripts help keep calls clear and produce useful answers.

Insurance agent or insurer

"My property at [address] had water damage on [date]. The water appears to have come from [rising floodwater / sewer backup / pipe leak / roof leak / unknown]. I need to know which policy or endorsement should respond, what documents you need before cleanup, and whether I should open separate building, contents, or backup claims."

Adjuster

"I have photos, videos, water-line evidence, receipts, estimates, and an itemized inventory. Before I remove materials or replace items, do you need additional photos or an inspection? Please confirm any instructions in writing."

Macomb assistance agency

"My household was affected by flooding at [address]. We need help with [electric/gas / water-sewer / food / hotel / rent / childcare / repairs]. I can provide proof of address, damage photos, bills or notices, receipts, and household information. Which program should I apply for first?"

Building department or floodplain administrator

"My property flooded and I need to repair [area/system]. Is this property in a flood hazard area or local flood-prone area? Do I need permits, inspections, substantial damage review, an elevation certificate, or code upgrades before work begins?"

Contractor

"Please separate your estimate into emergency mitigation, repair, code-required work, and resilience upgrades. Include photos, moisture readings if available, materials to remove, materials to replace, labor, permits, and any safety concerns."
